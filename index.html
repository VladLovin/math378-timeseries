Data Preparation

The Amazon sales dataset contains 50,000 individual transactions spanning January 2022 through December 2023. Because ARMA-family models require equally-spaced observations, transaction-level records were aggregated into 24 monthly total revenue observations — the target variable for all subsequent analysis.

Finding

The series shows no obvious long-run trend — revenue oscillates around a relatively stable mean of ~$1.37M/month. There is meaningful month-to-month variation (range: $1.24M – $1.46M), but no clear upward or downward drift, which already hints that the series may be stationary in levels.

02 ——

Stationarity Assessment

A stationary series has a constant mean, variance, and autocorrelation structure over time — a requirement for ARMA modeling. Two complementary tests were applied: the ADF test (H₀: unit root exists, i.e., non-stationary) and the KPSS test (H₀: series is stationary). Using both reduces the risk of a wrong conclusion from either alone.

The ADF statistic of −5.894 falls far below all critical values (1%: −3.77, 5%: −3.00, 10%: −2.64), providing strong evidence against a unit root.

03 ——

ACF and PACF Analysis

The ACF measures correlation between the series and its own lags. The PACF measures that correlation after removing the influence of all shorter lags. Together they identify the ARMA order: ACF cuts off at lag q + PACF tails off → MA(q); PACF cuts off at lag p + ACF tails off → AR(p). The 95% confidence band is ±1.96/√n = ±0.409 for this series.

Autocorrelation Function (ACF)
Partial Autocorrelation Function (PACF)

Reading the Plots → Model Order

The ACF shows a significant spike at lag 1 (−0.234) followed by rapid decay into the confidence band — a pattern consistent with a cutoff after lag 1. The PACF tails off gradually across multiple lags rather than cutting off sharply. This ACF-cuts-off / PACF-tails-off pattern is the textbook signature of an MA(1) process, suggesting q = 1 and p = 0. This reading was confirmed by auto_arima's AIC-based search in Section 4.

04 ——

ARMA Modeling and Forecasting

An ARIMA(0, 0, 1) model with intercept was fit using auto_arima (AIC-minimizing stepwise search) and confirmed against the ACF/PACF reading. The model expresses monthly revenue as a constant mean plus a fraction of the previous month's forecast error. Residual diagnostics and a 6-month forward forecast are presented below.

05 ——

Summary of Results

The full DataCamp pipeline — stationarity assessment, ACF/PACF order identification, ARIMA fitting, and residual validation — was successfully applied to Amazon monthly revenue. Each step informed the next, producing a defensible, interpretable model.

Step 1 — Data Prep
50,000 transactions aggregated into 24 monthly observations of total revenue for equally-spaced time series modeling.
Step 2 — Stationarity
ADF test: p = 0.0000, stat = −5.894. Stationary in levels. No differencing required (d = 0).
Step 3 — ACF / PACF
ACF cuts off at lag 1 (−0.234); PACF tails off. Classic MA(1) signature → q = 1, p = 0.
Step 4 — Model
ARIMA(0,0,1) + intercept. AIC = 597.23. MAPE = 3.27%. Residuals confirmed white noise.
Step 5 — Forecast
6-month forecast converges to $1.37M/month (the series mean), with 95% CIs of ±$114K reflecting short-series uncertainty.
Key Limitation
With only 24 observations, confidence intervals are wide. A longer history would yield narrower, more actionable intervals.
